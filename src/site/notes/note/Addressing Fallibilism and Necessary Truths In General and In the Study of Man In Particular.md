A common argument against [[G1/The Necessary-Contingent False Dichotomy\|the claim that we can know necessary truths]], [[note/The Kantian Attack on Cognition\|is that such a claim implies human infallibility]]. However, this is not the case. It is crucial to clarify that recognizing necessary truths does not require one to hold that human beings are infallible. Instead, three key observations must be considered to understand how fallibilism and the recognition of necessary truths, particularly the axiom of human action, can coexist without contradiction:

**First**, [[note/The Error of Conflating the Potential with the Actual\|being aware that you may be mistaken doesn't mean that you are aware that you are mistaken]]. It means that you have a concrete reason to *suppose* that you may be mistaken in this case. **Second**, [[note/The Attack on the Senses from their Limitation\|fallibilism and necessity are perfectly compatible]]—Fallibilism is a thesis about our *liability to error*, not a thesis about the modal status (*possible falsity*) of what we believe. **Third**, neither [[note/The Primacy of Existence vs The Primacy of Consciousness\|essentialism in philosophy]] nor [[Logic\|the what-logic]] that is associated with it necessarily involves any such pretensions to [[G1/Context\|infallibility and knowledge]]. One need not claim infallibility or hold to an extreme dogmatism, in order to hold that the axiom of [[note/Human Action\|human action]] is a necessary truth—moreover [[note/Fact\|there is no need to interpret fallibilism so strongly that it conflicts with holding that the axiom is a necessary truth]]. 

But, *how exactly is the axiom of human action grounded in human nature*? *How does that work*? Consider the [[note/Definition\|definition]] of human beings as a "*rational animal*." This defines the nature of a human being. [[note/The Role of Definition for a Conceptual Being\|It notes the overall limits of human nature]] and is [[G1/Conceptual File Folder\|like a title on a file folder that directs and sorts one's investigations]], though it does not state all the [[Characteristics vs. Properties\|characteristics]] of human nature. The [[genus\|genus]] "*animal*" [[note/Conceptual Order\|places humans within the broader category]] of living organisms that possess certain [[note/Consciousness is a Biological Faculty\|biological characteristics such as sense-perception, motility, and emotional response to stimuli]], and so everything that is true of being an animal is also true of a human being. Moreover, what is true of each of these considerations is true as well of a human being.  The [[differentia\|differentia]], rationality, is determined not only by reference to what [[note/Differentiation\|differentiates]] a human being from other animals but by [[note/Conceptual Common Denominator\|what does so most fundamentally]]. That is, **it is the feature upon which the other necessary features are dependent**. "*Rational*" specifies the unique [[G2/ppp/Capacities and Capabilities\|capacity]] of humans for [[note/Mental Focus\|selective mental focus]], [[note/Abstraction\|abstract reasoning]], and [[note/Integration\|Integration]] of the various parts of their being into a whole, an actor that which engages in purposeful behavior.

A human being is rational, meaning human beings have a distinctive faculty of awareness. Humans have a conceptual capacity that fundamentally involves [[note/The Objectivist Theory of Concepts\|the power to grasp the world in conceptual terms]], it enables humans to identify and integrate [[note/Sense Perception\|the data provided by their senses]], forming concepts and making [[Value-Judegment\|judgments that guide their actions]]. This includes the ability to [[Classificatory Propositions\|form classifications]], [[G2/ppp/The Special Nature of Theoretical Knowledge in the Study of Man\|develop theories]], [[G1/Experiment\|formulate hypotheses]], [[note/Inference\|derive conclusions]], [[new 2/The Validity of Introspection\|reflect on various subjects (be they past, present, or future)]], [[Ends and Values\|identify goals]], [[Means and Ends\|evaluate means to achieve them]], and make planned, deliberate choices. This capacity is expressed in two basic forms *speculative reasoning* (the pursuit of [[note/Truth\|truth]]) and *practical reasoning* (the pursuit of [[Life as the Standard of Value\|human good]]). Rationality, understood broadly as a conceptual *capacity*, is the [[G1/Fundamentality\|fundamental]] modality by which [[Value vs Fact\|human beings consider and take on the issues of human life]]—giving rise to [[note/Human Action\|human action]]—**it is the fundamental operating feature of the human life form or nature**. 

To help concretize this human faculty of reason(i.e. abstraction and integration) take the [[Socrates\|Socrates]] is mortal [[G1/Syllogism\|Syllogism]], which is a logical structure that is, in one sense, mathematical. It equates a root, *men*, with a square, *mortality*, and a number, *Socrates*, with a root, *men*, and finds that the number is equal to the square. [[note/Consciousness as Naturalistic\|This structure is ultimately neurological]]. To state "*all men are mortal*" requires that the brain can hold information about men *and* mortality *and* can put them together **or imagine them as a part**. The mechanisms by which we do this is based on the structure of the brain regions that encode this information. The brain can be thought of consisting of regions where the sense organs are taking in the information for processing. *Each of these sense organs then comes together, with their perception of [[note/Fact\|the facts of reality]], and integrates into a larger perception stored in memory somewhere in the brain*. 

These patterns of [[thought\|thought]], [[note/Generalization\|generalization]], and [[note/Integration\|integration]] are [[note/Self-Evident vs. Obvious\|evident]] in our cognitive processes. Maps, tree diagrams, [[note/Proposition\|propositions]], and [[note/Concept-Formation as an Algebraic Process\|algebraic expressions]] are forms of general structures filled with [[G1/Abstract vs. Concrete\|concretes]]. **Thus, the patterns of thought in metaphors and analogies result from how we encode knowledge**. The stimuli we receive are processed in a few brain regions, necessitating general structures to handle various [[note/Relationship (BPC)\|relationships]]. The [[note/Conceptual Common Denominator\|conceptual common denominator]] limit (CCDL), [[note/Addressing the Attacks on Abstraction In General and In the Study of Man In Particular#^06ff81\|as previously explained]], reflects this representational mapping of information on the cortex. The brain's regions involved in integrating spatial and non-spatial cognitive maps of the world are [[note/Metaphysically Given\|metaphysically given]]. [[note/Percept\|Percepts]], [[note/Concept\|concepts]], [[Mathematics\|mathematics]], and all other [[The Epistemology of Tool-Use\|tools used in cognition]] comes from concretes that have been processed through these physical mechanisms, such as the entorhinal cortex and the hippocampal region, where two integrating mechanisms function: one creates a general map, and the other fills that map with concrete information. These regions allow for the formation of maps that allow one *to [[note/Inference\|move in thought from one thought to another]]*, enabling the abstraction and [[note/Measurement\|measurement]] *necessary* for [[note/The Objectivist Theory of Concept Formation as Valid\|concept formation]]. For example, one brain region holds that [[note/Unit (Epistemology)\|one unit]] is one unit, no matter where they are on the planet, and you can take four steps to move one unit. It can conceptualize that *moving north* ↥, *east* ↱, *south* ↴, and *west* ↵, *one unit each* <span style="display: inline-block; position: relative; width: 1.25em; height: 2em; vertical-align: middle;"> <span style="font-size: 1.1em; display: inline-block;">⬜</span> <span style="position: absolute; bottom: 0.07em; left: 0.05em; font-size: 1.25em;">↙</span> </span>  ‎ will get you to stop right where you started. *Equiping us with a cognitive map that represents movement between [[note/Epistemic Units as Objective\|concrete perceptual units]]*, generalizing the tasks of the brain. The other region *[[G1/Memory\|remembers]] concretes and, given a map, can place them around the environment to help navigate*. This mapping structure provides [[Conditional Hierarchy vs. Inductive Hierarchy\|a hierarchy of knowledge]], proving why it is fundamentally just as easy to *move up* the inductive hierarchy as it is to *move down* the deductive hierarchy. Which can be thought of as traversing(making inferences) in these two-way streets(propositions) on a map with a grid of nodes:

This is possible *due to the way in which the brain regions physically exist*. Without getting into too much specific details, we can work out the *necessary* factors: (**1**) The brain is composed of task-specific regions. (**2**) [[note/The Epistemic Role of Measurement\|The brain has a finite amount of of material, and as such necessarily also has a finite amount of information processing power and a finite amout of information storage capacity]]. (**3**) Therefore each specific region has *limited* processing power and storage capacity. (**4**) [[G1/Conceptual File Folder\|An unlimited number of concepts can be formed, and an unlimited number of entries can be added to a conceptual file folder]]. (**5**) Therefore each specific region *must have the ability to generalize its task while also allowing for particular [[note/The Epistemic Sense of Objectivity\|objects]] or concepts*. 

What we notice here, then, is that the brain has some specific regions which, through their physical structure, create *a general pattern for integration and differentiation* and will create *a means of placing specific objects in those patterns*. [[note/The Epistemic Primacy of Sense Perception\|These patterns will be right in front of our eyes]]—they will be the same patterns that we use [[note/The Role of Language for a Conceptual Being\|to think out loud and use to share thoughts with others]]. When you [[new 2/The Validity of Introspection\|introspect ]]on such things, you find that *maps are a general structure that we fill with concretes*. Other such regions do the equivalent of tree modeling, again that being a general structure that we fill with concretes. Since all of the stimulus we receive is processed in *the same handful of brain regions* with limited processing power and storage capacity, those regions will have *no room to construct a new spatial coordinate system or a new non-spatial mapping system or a new category tree for every single relationship*. There is thus, this [[G1/Man's Capacity to Reason\|faculty of reasoning]] that takes in [[note/All Knowledge is Either Perceptual or Conceptual\|all percepts and/or concepts]], and lays them out into a hexagonal pattern, and [[G1/The Objectivist Theory of Similarity\|compares the common denominators]] to determine if this object belongs to the concept or not, *that is fundamental to man's nature as a rational animal*. 

You [[note/Measurement Relationships\|measure the distance between nodes]] by means of [[note/Proposition\|propositions]] and this allows for [[note/Inference\|inference]] or *movement in thought from one thought to another*. The process is similar physically to mathematical graphing, [[G1/The Objectivist Theory of Similarity\|with distances acting as factors in the similarity between objects and concepts]], and hence why [[G3/Zzz/Principles of Metanormative Justice\|abstract notions of "just" or "un-just"]] can exist or "[[The Virtue of Justice\|my sense of justice]] is near [[The Virtue of Rational Honesty\|my sense of honesty]]." In this representational CCDL space, justice and honesty can be situated right next to each other. *The problem with moral issues is how the concepts were [[Induction\|induced]]*, such that some people might view man as "*naturally evil*" and therefore "*[[Benevolent Universe Premise vs. Malevolent Universere Premise\|cosmic justice]]*" might mean dishonesty is closer to justice. 

And with that extensive [[note/A Physical Explanation for Consciousness or Free Will does not Negate Their Existence\|physical explanation]] through, let us now consider three statements by [[Murray N. Rothbard\|Rothbard]] that illustrate this view of human nature:

1. "*Human beings possess a rational consciousness. [[note/Consciousness is a Biological Faculty\|Stones, molecules, and planets cannot choose their courses]]. . . . Only human beings possess [[note/Free Will\|free will]] and [[note/Consciousness\|consciousness]], for they are conscious and they can and indeed must choose their course of action. To ignore this primordial fact about the nature of man—to ignore his volition, his free will—is to misconstrue [[note/Fact\|the facts of reality]] and therefore to be [[note/Psuedoscience\|profoundly and radically unscientific]]*." — Rothbard

2. "*[[note/The Axiom of Consciousness\|The fundamental axiom for the study of man is the existence of individual consciousness]]. [[note/Epistemology as Dependent Upon the Fallibility of a Conceptual Consciousness\|Not being omniscient, a man must learn]]. [[note/Consciousness is an Active Process\|He must ever adopt ideas and act upon them]], choosing [[Means and Ends\|ends and the means to attain these ends]]*." — Rothbard

3. "*[[Finishedbaby/Epilology\|The true science of man]] bases itself upon the existence of individual human beings, upon individual life and consciousness. . . . [[G2/ppp/Methodological Individualism\|The true science of man concentrates on the individual]] as of central [[note/Epistemology\|epistemological]] and [[Ethics\|ethical]] importance*." — Rothbard


Rothbard appeals to an array of [[note/Fact\|facts]] about human beings. This [[note/Empiricism\|knowledge is based on sense perception]], but it is *not* the result of any [[Particular\|particular experience]] or [[G1/Experiment\|experiment]]. Instead, it is based on **general experience that nearly everyone possesses**. Rothbard is also following [[Saint Thomas Aquinas\|Aquinas]] in [[note/The Locus of Human Free Will\|linking free will and rational consciousness]]. As Aquinas states, "*Man is master of his actions through his reason and will, whence too the free will is defined as the faculty and will of reason*. [[note/Mental Focus\|Self-direction and the exercise of reason]] are not separate acts of two isolated capacities but distinct aspects of the same [[note/Conscious Action\|conscious act]]. Human reason is, in its very *essence*, a self-directed activity—[[G1/Consciousness as Irreducible\|consciousness is irreducible]]. 

The brain's existence depends on coming together through complex physical gradients, but the result—consciousness—[[note/Consciousness as a non-Entity\|is not itself physical]]. Consciousness is a mental phenomenon. Mental activities allow for [[note/Entity and Property (BPC)\|the contemplation of physical entities]], [[note/Measurement Relationships\|sometimes disregarding their inherent rules or properties]], but [[note/Fallacy of Reification of the Zero\|consciousness cannot imagine or pretend that nothingness exists]]. *It can only replace all things with an empty room or a dark void*, stressing the necessity of grounding human action in reality. The axiom of human action is rooted in observable reality, it is not merely a rationalistic unpacking of the concept "*human being*." [[note/The Metaphysical Status of Concepts\|Concepts and definitions, like consciousness]], are [[note/Consciousness Has an Object and a Subject\|not independent or self-sufficent existents but are relationships between man and reality]]. Consciousness is able to learn about new [[note/Fact\|facts of reality]] and, in fact, was a [[Tabula Rasa\|blank canvas when it first came into being]]. [[Shapes\|Shapes]], [[Quantitative\|quantities]], [[note/The Metaphysical Status of Sensory Qualities\|textures, smells, and colors, etc]]., were first [[note/Sensation\|detected]], then [[note/Integration\|integrated]], then stored in the brain somewhere, somehow. [[G1/Early Perceptual Development\|Over time, experiences become familiar and locked in, reinforcing the knowledge base]].

[[Animals\|Animals]], through [[note/The Sensory-Perceptual Level as Automatic\|automatic perceptual integrations]], retrieve stored information and process it in various brain regions. The brain's capacity for processing is finite, *necessitating* [[note/Free Will\|a mechanism for consciousness to decide]], *not the brain*, to *decide* which regions of the brain to [[note/Integration\|synchronize together]] in order to get *the desired outcome*, particularly in *practical reasoning* tasks. **This means that consciousness must have a volitional nature to decide which circuits of the brain to wire together and to decide if the results the brain automatically generated were useful or not**. As Rothbard states, "*Man's necessity to choose means that at any given time he is acting to bring about some end in the immediate or distant future, that is, that he has purposes. The steps he takes to achieve his ends are his means. Man is born with no innate knowledge of what ends to choose or how to use which means to achieve them. Having no inborn knowledge of how to survive and prosper, he must learn what ends and means to adopt, and he is **liable** to make errors along the way. But only his reasoning mind can show him his goals and how to attain them*." In order for consciousness to be capable of making a decision, it must be aware of the difference. In fact, consciousness is a form of awareness of two or more **options**, In other words. . . . "*consiousness is a difference-detector*." Not all consciousnesses are capable of fixing their situation when presented externally with only the bad option, but all consciousnesses will have some response to that awareness. *Whether the decision is automatic or volitional, it is not random nor is it deterministic, as consciousness can choose not to respond*.

[[note/The Levels of Consciousness\|The higher the level of consciousness]], the better it is at *acting* against [[note/The Determinist-Indeterminist False-Dichotomy\|those biasing factors that make determinism so appealing]]. Biasing factors, also known as physical gradients of an obect's nature, are what inanimate objects have no choice but to follow without the ability to do anything else. Despite its reliance on the physical brain, consciousness is distinct from brain processes, much like a magnetic field generated by an electric current is distinct from the current itself. Thus, while brain actions are *necessary* [[note/Conditioning Connections (BPC)\|conditions]] for consciousness, *they are not identical to consciousness*. **Fundamentally, humans have no choice about what they essentially are, and it is upon that fact that the axiom of human action rests**. Finally, this account of the action axiom requires recognizing that, as living beings, [[G2/ppp/A Theory of the Analogy Between Epilogical Phenomena and Natural Organisms\|human beings are teleological entities]]. Human reason and volition—and thus human action—are end-oriented. **Humans have no choice about being end-oriented or about the need to choose ends. Nor do they have any choice about whether reason is to be employed to attain worthwhile lives. Humans must direct their reason to understand the world and achieve various ends or goals they think worthy of pursuit**. 

Regardless of how the justification of the rest of the [[note/Proposition\|propositions]] of [[note/Praxeology\|praxeology]] and [[G2/ppp/Economics Nature and Its Uses\|economic theorizing]] might fare, regardless of how we go on and try to consider the application of [[Principles of Economics\|economic principles]] to particular situations, *these objective foundations of praxeology* provide a viable and powerful alternative to positivist, constructivist, and neopragmatist accounts. It allows for the possibility that there are [[G2/ppp/The Special Nature of Theoretical Knowledge in the Study of Man\|economic laws, not merely empirical generalizations, and that these laws are necessary truths reflective of reality and are thus not merely tautologies or human constructs]]. This account provides a foundational understanding of human action for both philosophy and economic theory, providing an indespensable framework for comprehending the purposive behavior that defines human existence.

Thus concludes the **final part** in [[note/The Objectivist Case for the Validation of Praxeology\|this objectivist case for the validity of praxeology]].
