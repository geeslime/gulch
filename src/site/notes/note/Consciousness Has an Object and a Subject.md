[[note/Consciousness\|Consciousness]] is a [[note/Relationship (BPC)\|relationship]] between a subject (the organism possesing the faculty of consciousness) and an object ([[Fact\|that which]] the organism is aware of). In other words, [[note/A Consciousness Conscious Only of Itself\|being conscious always involves being conscious of some*thing*]]. This basic idea underpins [[note/Four Fundamentals Characteristics of Consciousness\|the characteristics of awareness]].

Awareness begs the question, *awareness of what?* Awareness *implies* an [[note/The Law of Identity\|object]] of awareness. [[note/The Attack on the Senses from their Limitation\|You can’t see without seeing something]], [[note/The Arbitrary as Neither True nor False\|nor can you think without thinking about something]]. [[note/The Levels of Consciousness\|Every act of awareness]] is *directed* towards an object, be it [[Abstract vs. Concrete\|concrete or abstract]].

> [[note/The Levels of Consciousness\|Every state of awareness]] involves some [[note/Conscious Content\|content]]. [[note/The Primacy of Existence vs The Primacy of Consciousness\|Awareness is, by definition, awareness of something]]. [[note/A Consciousness Conscious Only of Itself\|A contentless state of consciousness is a contradiction in terms]]. [^1]

Even when imagining *something that doesn’t exist*, like a golden mountain, there is still [[note/Conscious Content\|conscious content]]. You’re not [[note/Fallacy of Reification of the Zero\|imagining a nothing]]; you're imagining a golden mountain, to imagine a golden mountain is to imagine that, not something else. The materials used in imagination come from one’s past [[note/Sense Perception\|perception]] of reality, here of [[note/The Metaphysical Status of Sensory Qualities\|golden qualia]] and of mountains; [[note/Hallucinations do not Negate the Validity of the Senses\|you can't imagine qualities you haven't experienced]]. Imagination is the ability to [[note/The Objectivist Theory of Concepts\|mentally combine and rearrange such materials]]. Imagination, [[note/The Sensory-Perceptual Level as Automatic\|in stark contrast to perception]], is under direct volitional control: you can [[vv/Visuospatial Sketchpad\|visualize]] whatever you like, but you can see only what *is* there to be seen. 

For instance, you can't visualize ultraviolet light because it's [[note/Different Forms of Sense Perception Lead to Different Capabilities in Deducing Laws\|beyond human visual perception]]. Similarly, those *blind from birth* don’t have visual imaginations, they cannot even remember shapes without having to recall the feeling of [[Particular\|particular]] objects in their hands. Imagination uses perceptual data [[Conceptual File Folder\|stored in memory]]. Imagining a friend’s face feels much like remembering it, because imagination is about reconfiguring stored perceptions.

Consciousness also [[note/Necessary Connections (BPC)\|necessitates]] a subject—the being that is aware. When [[note/Concepts of Consciousness as Involving Measurement-Omission\|forming the concept of "consciousness,"]] one reflects on personal mental actions (seeing, hearing, thinking, etc.) and integrates these with similar activities observed in other [[note/Man\|men]]. Though [[note/Consciousness as Unscientific Because it Cannot be Defined\|we can't directly experience another's consciousness]], we [[note/Inference\|infer]] it by analogy to our own experiences.

Philosophers like [[David Hume\|David Hume]] questioned how we become aware of the self, saying that [[vv/The Validity of Introspection\|introspection]] only reveals [[Abstract vs. Concrete\|concrete]] [[note/Conscious Action\|mental acts]], not a [[Character\|"self"]]. The response is that *it's you who is introspecting*, observing your consciousness in action. We don’t first discover general consciousness and then attach it to a person; instead, we start with our own consciousness and [[note/Conceptual Common Denominator\|recognize similar actions in others]].

[[people/Ayn Rand\|Rand]] summarizes:

> [[vv/existence/Clarifying ''Existence Exists''\|Existence exists]] — and understanding [[note/The Fundamental Axioms of Metaphysics\|this statement implies two corollary axioms]]: something exists (**the object**) which one perceives, and one exists (**the subject**) who perceives. Consciousness is the faculty of perceiving that which exists. Without something to be conscious of, there can be no consciousness. [^2]

[^2]: [AS, 1015]
