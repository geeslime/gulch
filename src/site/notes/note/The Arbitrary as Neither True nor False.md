Granted that [[note/Dismissing the Arbitrary\|the arbitrary does not qualify as knowledge]], what is the [[note/Epistemology\|epistemological]] status of [[note/Arbitrary\|the arbitrary]]?

In cases like [[note/Past Lives as an Example of The Arbitrary\|Shirley MacLaine and her past lives]] or [[note/Johnny Cochran at the O.J. Trial Claiming a Police Conspiracy\|Johnny Cochran in his police conspiracy wiles]], what is common is their [[note/Evasion\|detachment]] from [[note/Evidence\|evidence]]. This detachment makes it impossible to [[note/Refute\|refute]], [[note/Proof\|prove]], [[Reason\|reason about]], or [[Scientific Method\|hypothesize these claims legitimately]]. Each of these arbitrary claims cannot be [[Thought\|cognitively processed]]—they are neither provable nor disprovable, making them *epistemologically useless.*

When confronted with [[note/Arbitrary\|arbitrary claims]], a rational mind becomes [[Cognitive Paralysis\|paralyzed]]. If and when you see that a claim is arbitrary, then you *cannot* think about its cognitive status, you *cannot* [[note/Differentiation\|weigh it]], [[note/Proposition\|assess it]], [[Cognitive Judgement\|judge]], [[note/Integration\|process]], [[note/Conceptual Order\|classify]], determine its [[probability\|probability]], its [[possibility\|possibility]], its [[note/Validation\|invalidity]], its [[note/Truth\|truth]], its [[note/Falsehood\|falsehood]], anything. *The mind stops, unable to move in any cognitive direction*.

The key is [[note/Integration\|integrating]] two [[Induction\|inductions]]: [[note/Arbitrary\|arbitrary claims are detached from evidence]], and therefore, arbitrary claims are detached from [[Thought\|thought]]. Claims detached from evidence are inherently detached from the possibility of thought. This leads to a crucial [[note/Epistemology\|epistemological]] conclusion: a claim inherently detached from thought *cannot* have any relationship to [[note/Existence Exists\|reality]]. If it *cannot* be processed cognitively, it *cannot* be said to be true, false, possible, or impossible.

True and false [[note/Proposition\|propositions]] involve a relationship between a [[note/Consciousness\|mind]] and reality. [[note/Truth\|True means correspondence to reality]], and [[note/Falsehood\|false means contradiction]]. Both concepts require a cognitive relationship with reality. [[note/Arbitrary\|Arbitrary]] claims, however, lack any relationship to reality, we can't [[note/Integration\|tie it]] to [[note/Fact\|Fact]], any way, for or against.

In sum, arbitrary claims lack cognitive and epistemological status, making them neither true nor false. [[note/Dismissing the Arbitrary\|They should be dismissed without thought]] to maintain the integrity of rational cognition and [[note/The Meaning of Objectivity\|objective]] reality.
