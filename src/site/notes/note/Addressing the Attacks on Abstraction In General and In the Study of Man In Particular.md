There are many objections to the [[note/Validation\|validity]] of [[note/Abstraction\|abstraction]] in [[Thought\|cognition]]: [[John Locke\|Locke]] says abstractions provide only a [[note/Floating Abstraction\|partial conception]] of [[note/Entity and Property (BPC)\|the nature of something]]. [[George Berkely\|Berkeley ]]denies that we can have [[note/The Objectivist Theory of Concepts\|abstract ideas]]. [[Immanuel Kant\|Kant]] says they [[note/The Invalidity of the Senses Implies The Invalidity of Knowledge\|cannot be based on sense perception]] but only imposed by the human mind. And many others have, in the 20th century, made [[note/Nominalism iff Sensualism\|similar charges against abstraction]]. The classic reply to this charge you'll often hear, **which itself presupposes a false dichotomy between thought and reality**, is that "*abstraction does not require either falsification or subtraction*." It holds that "*even though [[note/The Primacy of Existence vs The Primacy of Consciousness\|our thought must answer to reality]] if we are to find truth, there is nonetheless [[G1/The Analytic-Synthetic False Dichotomy\|a difference between thinking something is thus and so and its being thus and so]]*." This suggests that abstraction must either falsify or accurately represent every detail of the object. The reality is, [[note/The Senses as Condensing Complex Information\|perception condenses the complex data provided by the senses]], and [[G1/Man's Capacity to Reason\|Man's Capacity to Reason]] (i.e. [[note/Abstraction\|abstraction]] and [[note/Integration\|integration]]) is what allows us to [[note/Conceptual Common Denominator\|grasp the essential nature of things]] *without* necessarily [[note/Measurement Relationships\|including every specific detail]]. 

Our [[G1/Knowledge\|knowledge]] must be of [[note/Fact\|reality]], [[note/The Metaphysical Status of Sensory Qualities\|but that does not mean that our knowledge is reality]]. Yet, if [[note/The Attack on the Senses by Eddington's Two Tables\|the way that things exist in thought is different from the way that things exist]]—[[note/Fact\|apart from their being thought about them]]—then how can we ever *avoid* [[note/Falsehood\|falsehood]]? The answer, of course, depends on [[G1/Abstract vs. Concrete\|the manner in which thought is different from the things that are thought about]]. As Anthony Kenny notes, "*To think a thing to be otherwise than it is is certainly to think falsely. But if all that is meant by thinking a thing other than it is is that the way it is with our thinking is different from the way it is with the thing that we are thinking about in its own existence, then there need be no falsehood involved. [...] To think that Henry VIII had no weight is to think a false thought, but there is no falsehood in thinking of Henry VIII without thinking of his weight. Henry VIII could never exist without having some weight or other, but a thought of Henry VIII can certainly exist without any thought at all about his weight*. This distinction supports the validity of abstract thought in understanding [[note/Human Action\|human action]] by allowing for [[note/Mental Focus\|selective focus]] on *relevant aspects* without the need of falsifying the nature of the entity or it's existence.

Moreover, there is then these [[G2/ppp/Two Basic Orientations of Theoretical Research\|two main orientations of the striving for cognition]]:

1. *Abstraction **Without** Precision (General Orientation)*: A type of thinking that involves considering the [[note/Matter and Form (BPC)\|form or character of something]] in an indeterminate manner. So that its individual differences **are not specified** but are nonetheless regarded as requiring specific determination. For example, the concept "*human being*."

2. *Abstraction **With** Precision (Individual Orientation)*: A type of thinking that separates or excludes specific determinations **from** the form or character of something. For example, the concept "*humanity*."

The first type of thinking is termed "*abstraction without precision*," or "*general orientation*" and the second is termed "*abstraction with precision*," or "*individual orientation*." For abstraction **without** precision, the form or character is **not considered** as related to its specific determination. For example, "*human being*." In the case of abstraction **with** precision, the form or character is considered **as not related** to its specific determination. For example, "*humanity*." As [[Saint Thomas Aquinas\|Aquinas]] notes, "*It is clear then that the essence of man is signified by two terms: "man" and "humanity," but in different ways. The term "man" **expresses it as a whole** because it does not prescind from the designation of [[note/Matter and Form (BPC)\|matter]] but contains it implicitly and indistinctly. This is why the term "man" [[note/Proposition\|can be predicated of individuals]]. But the term "humanity" **signifies the essence as a part** because it includes [[G2/ppp/Capacities and Capabilities\|only what belongs to man as man]], prescinding from all designation of matter. As a result, it cannot be predicated of individual men*." (See: [[note/Measurement Relationships\|Measurement Relationships]])

[[note/The Role of Definition for a Conceptual Being\|Deciding where to delineate our definitions for concepts and determining if information belongs to a concept is a capability of our consciousness]]. We understand that [[note/Four Fundamentals Characteristics of Consciousness\|fundamental to the character of consciousness]] is that [[note/Consciousness\|consciousness is an active process]], always aware of [[note/Consciousness Has an Object and a Subject\|itself and the external world]]. It must engage with existence [[note/The Primacy of Existence vs The Primacy of Consciousness\|since it cannot identify itself without first experiencing something outside itself]]. And that [[note/Consciousness is a Biological Faculty\|consciousness must learn about the world, other conscious agents, inanimate objects, and the spatial and temporal relations between them]]. This active engagement is what allows humans to make deliberate and informed decisions. Identity formation is only an adolescent problem because [[G1/Early Perceptual Development\|it takes years of interacting with reality to develop]] a coherent [[new 2/The Validity of Introspection\|sense of self]]. Thus, since consciousness is specifically an interaction with the existing world, [[note/A Physical Explanation for Consciousness or Free Will does not Negate Their Existence\|it cannot be reduced to non-consciousness]].

So, then we can abstractly consider the form or character—that is, [[note/Characteristics vs. Properties (BPC)\|the characteristics of individual human beings and their properties]], say [[people/Ayn Rand\|Ayn Rand]], [[Murray N. Rothbard\|Murray Rothbard]], and [[note/LiquidZulu\|Liquid Zulu]]—*without precision*. When we do so, we are considering their form or character *indeterminately* as a [[note/Unit (Epistemology)\|conceptual unit or universal]]. But we know nonetheless that their form or character must have *some determination*. [[note/Consciousness as a non-Entity\|Consciousness is distinct from the brain]], or [[note/Objectivism does not Hold that Consciousness is an Entity\|conscious organ]], as demonstrated by scenarios where consciousness is absent despite brain functionality. For example, during surgeries requiring anesthesia which inhibits the functionality of the patient's consciousness while the brain remains operational. This suggests that [[note/Consciousness as Naturalistic\|consciousness interacts with the brain physically, allowing for volition]]. Volition, [[note/The Four Causes (BPC)\|a type of causation]], is the [[note/Conditioning Connections (BPC)\|conditioning connection]] between an [[note/Action (BPC)\|action]] and the form or character of the object acting(a formal cause). [[note/The Locus of Human Free Will\|Human nature includes the capacity for volition, along with reason]]; [[note/The Primary Choice Being Focus or Drift\|it's ultimately by focusing or tuning out]] individuals [[Action as an Exchange\|decide what course of action they'll take]]. The grasping of this [[note/Fact\|fact]] involves *abstraction without precision* as we don't know, or aren't considering, the [[note/The Four Causes (BPC)\|materal cause]] of man's actions. We aren't considering how this might distinguish the [[Character\|conscious character]] of different individuals from eachother, but we must recognize [[note/Existence is Identity\|the necessity of its existence]].

To abstract in this sense is "*not to subtract*"—that is, it is not to reduce one's awarness to part of an object but to grasp it as a whole. We can say of each of these individuals they are "*human beings*," but clearly we cannot say of each that they are "*humanity.*" **Abstraction without precision then is neither to falsify nor to lessen our apprehension of what is encountered through sense [[note/Sense Perception\|perception]]**. For, as said, the form or character—when so considered—is not excluded or cut off from its individual differences, which are treated as implicit. Indeed, the general-orientated manner of abstraction enhances our apprehension and allows us to discover what human beings *essentially* are. [[note/The Empiricist Case for the Action Axiom\|The Empiricist view of the action axiom]] depends on *abstraction without precision*. It does not positively exclude individual differences of individual human beings but instead treats these differences as implicit or given. These differences only become explicit when each instance of what is signified by the concept "*human being*" is considered. For instance: "*Rand*;" "*Rothbard*;" "*Zulu*." 

*What is common to these individuals must be expressed determinately in their concrete forms or characters of these individuals*. To determine if a particular object falls within the [[note/Definition\|definition]] of a [[note/Concept\|concept]] and can be a member of that concept, [[note/Conceptual Common Denominator\|the object must share the same units and fall within an acceptable boundary]]. This can be formulated as a [[Mathematics\|mathematical]] limit. The object belongs to the concept as its characteristics approach the concept's characteristics. *For every unit that exists, there must be a corresponding unit in the object*. If the difference in characteristics is less than the *common denominator*, the object belongs to the concept, and the error margin is within an [[note/The Epistemic Sense of Objectivity\|objective]] range. This conceptual common denominator limit provides [[note/Concept-Formation as an Algebraic Process\|a mathematical formulation of similarity among entities]]. Each concept, held together with mathematical precision, functions as elements of [[Algerbra\|algebraic]] expressions. Algebra [[Reduction\|reduces]] and [[note/Integration\|balances]] concepts, finding [[note/Conditioning Connections (BPC)\|connections]] between [[note/Unit (Epistemology)\|units]]. Similarly to mathematics, [[note/The Role of Language for a Conceptual Being\|language and concept formation seek to distill concepts to their core]].
{ #06ff81}


[[G3/poop/Conflicts\|Crusoe's spear]], for example, can be reduced to its base units:

- This spear has a shaft.
- This spear has a head.
- The shaft is made of wood.
- The head is made of stone.
- The wood is cylindrical.
- The stone is arrow-shaped.
- [[note/Measurement\|The cylinder is 2/3 of a finger across and four feet in length]].
- The stone weighs as much as a cow's skull filled with water.

These [[percepts\|percepts]] abstracted from objects [[note/Differentiation\|differentiate]] objects and [[note/Epistemic Units as Objective\|reduce them to perceptual units]]. Allowing for [[note/Abstraction\|abstract differentiation]] and for us to consider concepts in mathematical terms.

We thus can say *what is common to human beings* and speak of human nature without either turning human nature into a universal part of individual human beings or treating abstraction as a process of [[subtraction\|subtraction]]. **It is from our conceptual grasp of human nature that the axiom of human action is justified**. One does not need to consult "*future experience*" to justify this claim because one is speaking of a *certain kind* of being and *how it acts*. One is not making an [[note/Inference\|inference]] from some to all but [[note/Proposition\|moving from a conceptual awareness of the nature of one thing to other instances of that same nature]]. [[note/The Objectivist Theory of Concepts\|Concepts are man-made products of abstracted percepts]] and [[note/Conceptual Order\|can be infinitely subcategorized and subdivided]]. This means every [[induction\|induction]] can be misinterpreted if removed from its proper [[G1/Context\|context]]. However, if the induction is correct, arguments that seem to contradict it in a *different context* will ultimately reinforce the original context's [[note/Validation\|validity]]. Whatever pertains to a human being according to his or her nature, regarding their [[capacities and capabilites\|capacities and capabilites]], will also be [[note/The Law of Identity\|true of every individual with that same nature]]. 


[[G1/The Analytic-Synthetic False Dichotomy\|There is then no principal difficulty in the action axiom being empirically known and being necessarily true]]. Because, the process of *abstraction without precision* requires [[note/The Law of Identity\|the natures of the things]] to determine [[note/The Role of Definition for a Conceptual Being\|what a concept signifies]]. What a concept signifies involves both an **intention*** which is *not limited to what is only explicitly considered or what is stated in a definition*, and an **extension** that *applies to all individuals that are instances of a certain kind, be they past, present, or future*. The basic [[Principles\|principle]] here is that *all natures are individualized and all individuals are natured*. This *general-orientated* approach curtails the view that the axiom of human action and the principles that result from it are *merely [[note/Generalization\|empirical generalizations]]*. This approach offers a basic alternative to [[G2/PRAXEOLOGY AS RATIONALISM’S STOLEN CONCEPT\|contemporary accounts of the action axiom]]. It provides the basis for applying [[Principles of Economics\|economic principles]] to [[note/Human Action\|human actions]] we [[note/Science\|observe so as to understand what is happening and why]]. Or, in other words, this approach provides a way for the [[note/Economics\|economist]] not to get lost in the so-called "*[[lectures/Austrian Alternatives to Conventional Economic Statistics\|empirical data]]*."

The [[note/Concept\|tools of human cognition]] cannot be known first and for themselves [[note/The Metaphysical Status of Concepts\|without there first being at least a logically prior cognition of something other than themselves]]. It is thus [[note/Epistemology as Dependent Upon the Fallibility of a Conceptual Consciousness\|not possible to hold that all awareness is at the very beginning and to the very end enclosed within itself]], as if it were [[note/Arbitrary\|one's private picture show or language]]. Thinking, then, is *an effortful, volitional process of combining identities that obey a causal nature by forming a mathematical formulation of similarities and differences of units identified within the object or concept*. Sometimes [[note/The Kantian Attack on Cognition\|it is argued that to claim that we can know necessary truths requires that we hold that human beings are infallible]]. ↯

But this is not the case.
